Alsace

Alsace is on the border with Germany and makes aromatic wines with their very own character and style. The grape variety is always clearly indicated on the label, which is rare in France, where otherwise origin is considered more important. The local food is somewhat rustic (choucroute/sauerkraut, stews etc) but goes surprisingly well with the elegant Riesling wines with varying degrees of sweetness. Other grape varieties of note are the rose-scented Gewurztraminer and the full-bodied Pinot Gris. The whites dominate, but you can find light and refreshing Pinot Noir wines.

Speed fact

  • Grapes:Riesling, Pinot gris, Gewurztraminer, Muscat and Pinot Noir
  • Taste of wine: Sweet but dry Riesling, rose-scented Gewurztraminer or light and refreshing Pinot Noir
  • Things to know: Influenced by German tradition and put the grapes on the lable

Burgundy

Burgundy begins with Chablis in the north, 125 miles south of Paris. Here, the relatively cool climate gives white wines from Chardonnay that are crispy and fresh. Further south, in the Côte d’Or, the whites, also from Chardonnay, are more full-bodied and oaky. The reds from Pinot Noir are very prestigious with prices to match. The best ones are exquisite and velvety. Burgundy ends with Beaujolais. Here, the Gamay grape gives fruity, fresh and often juicy and easy-drinking wines.

Speed fact

  • Grapes:Grapes: Chardonnay, Pinot Noir and Gamay
  • Taste of white wine: Dry fresh white wine from Chablis, Buttery oak flavor white wine from Côte d’Or
  • Taste of red wines: Light and acidity Pinot Noir wines or fruity and fresh from the Gamay grape.
  • Things to know: If the vineyard's name is on the label, it is usually a sign that it is high quality

Champagne

The world’s most famous sparkling wines are made from grapes that grow in a rather tricky climate in northern France. The upside is the high acidity which is paramount for high-quality sparkling wine. The grapes that are allowed are Pinot Noir, Chardonnay and Pinot Meunier. The bubbles are formed during the second fermentation in the bottle. This way to make sparkling wine is called the méthode champenoise in Champagne and méthode traditionelle, the traditional method, elsewhere.

Speed fact

  • Grapes: Chardonnay, Pinot Noir and Pinot Meunier
  • Taste of wine: high acidity, with small bubbels and a taste of miniral, citrus and cheese
  • Things to know: This is where they started making sparkling wine

Loire

Known for its white wines, mainly Sancerre, Vouvray, Pouilly-Fume and Muscadet. The Loire Valley rests on the northwest side of France, beginning just inland from the Atlantic and running the length of the Loire River. The wines from the Loire Valley come in a vast array of styles, from dry to sweet, and from predominantly white to sparkling. Wines from the Loire are often, but not always, crafted in a lighter style due in part to the region's cooler climate. Styles to keep an eye out for include Pouilly-Fume (the most concentrated regional wine bringing a fuller bodied white made from Sauvignon Blanc grapes), Sancerre (typically medium-bodied and also made from Sauvignon Blanc), Muscadet (named for the region, gives a lighter-styled white wine made from the Melon de Bourgogne grape), and Vouvray (from the Chenin Blanc grape).

Speed fact

  • Grapes: Sauvignon Blanc, Melon de Bourgogne and Chenin Blanc.
  • Taste of wine: High acidity, with taste of mineral, citrus and nettles
  • Things to know: This wines goes great with goatcheese
